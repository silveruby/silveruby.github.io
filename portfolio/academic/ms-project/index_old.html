Master's Project - Touchscreen For the Visually Impaired

Phase 1: Research

Research Question

I decided to research about touchscreen for the visually impaired because I am interested in multimodal interactions as well as accessibility.

Touchscreen accessibility for the visually impaired users is challenging because there is no tactile controls. From speaking with the visually impaired community, many enjoy objects that provide ample tactile control. Therefore, I wanted to know if users would prefer to use tactile control over the existing gesture control.

Phase 2: Study Design

Creating the Study

  • Performances Vs. Preferences
  • Qualitative & Quantitative Data
  • Within-Subject Study On Hierarchical Menu

In order to compare two different types of control style, I had to figure out a way to create a solid study. The study should be designed in such way that it would allow me to collect both quantitative and qualitative data about user preferences and performances.

I decided to create a study where the users have to navigate through a hierarchical menu. The user have to navigate through a hierarchical menu to search for target items. The study was within-subject so that a user needed to navigate the menu using both conditions: gesture and tactile. Through this, I collected user's performance through task completion time as well as their subjective preferences through questionnaires.

Phase 4: Evaluation

Calculating the Quantitative Data

  • Six Participants, 1hr Each
  • Average Time: Gesture-42.25s, Tactile-47.19s
  • Learning Curve Between 1st and 2nd Condition

I went through the study with six participants from the Center for Visually Impaired at Atlanta. They range from age 20s-50s. On average, there is a ~+20% learning curve between 1st and 2nd condition. Four successfully complete the entire study. The Average completion time from the participants were 42.25s for gesture and 47.19s for tactile.

Analyzing the Qualitative Data

  • Participants With Diabetes
  • Problem with Tactile Control
  • Difficulty in Learning the Hierarchical Menu

Most users preferred the gesture control. One told me after the study that he is more comfortable with gesture because he has problem with the tactile control. It turned out that this participant had diabetes and one of the symptoms is losing sense of touch.

Through this study, I conclude that although tactile control can be helpful for visually impaired users for many tasks, gesture control is actually more powerful for them to use touchscreen.

Providing Guidelines

  • Physical buttons should be for quick tasks/shortcuts
  • Physical buttons should be small and easy to find
  • Gestures provide quick navigation
  • Better to remember different gestures than spent time looking for physical (tactile) buttons
  • Combination should be the best
